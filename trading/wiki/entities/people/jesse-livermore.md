# Jesse L. Livermore

## Snapshot

Jesse L. Livermore was a prominent early twentieth-century speculator whose 1940 book [How to Trade in Stocks](../../source-notes/2026-06-13-how-to-trade-in-stocks-jesse-livermore.md) presents a trading philosophy centered on price records, time, pivotal points, market leadership, patience, and capital preservation. His earlier career is chronicled in Edwin LeFevre's 1923 classic [Reminiscences of a Stock Operator](../../source-notes/2026-06-19-reminiscences-of-a-stock-operator.md), narrated through the fictional alter ego "Larry Livingston."

## Career Arc (from Reminiscences)

- **Bucket-shop origins.** Started as a quotation-board boy around age 14, kept a "dope book" of price behaviour, and became so consistently profitable betting on fluctuations that bucket shops across New England and the Midwest barred him — earning the nickname "the Boy Plunger."
- **Repeated ruin and recovery.** His bucket-shop scalping method failed in real Stock Exchange houses (execution lag, his own market impact). He went broke several times before age 30, each time returning to first principles rather than a new system.
- **The May 9, 1901 lesson.** Correct on direction but wiped out because the printed tape lagged the floor by 30–40 points during the Northern Pacific panic — the formative lesson that tape reading alone is insufficient.
- **First million (1906–1907).** A Union Pacific "hunch" in 1906 and a disciplined short campaign into the 1907 panic produced his first $1M+, made by trading *basic conditions* rather than tips.
- **The Percy Thomas disaster (~1908).** Allowed a magnetic cotton authority to talk him out of his own bearish read; reversed to the bull side, averaged down a loser, and lost nearly his entire fortune — the costliest lesson in protecting independent judgment.
- **Later mastery.** Returned to form (the 1915 Bethlehem Steel trade) and was repeatedly hired to conduct legitimate stock-distribution campaigns (e.g., Imperial Steel, Consolidated Stove).

**Biographical note:** Later editions of *Reminiscences* prepend photo captions describing Livermore's wealth, family tragedies, and 1940 suicide; these are editorial framing, not part of LeFevre's 1923 text.

## Trading Philosophy

- Treat speculation as a business, not an entertainment or a source of quick money.
- Wait for the market to confirm the thesis through price action.
- Focus on major movements and avoid being distracted by minor fluctuations.
- Add only to positions that are already working.
- Exit when market behavior gives a danger signal.
- Keep records so the trader can act from evidence rather than tips, hope, or broker pressure.

## Caveats

The 1940 book reflects a different market structure, commission environment, regulatory regime, and price scale. Its principles may be durable, but the exact thresholds and examples require modernization before practical use.
