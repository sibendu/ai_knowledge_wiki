# Trading Psychology

Trading psychology covers behavioral patterns that affect trading decisions, including overtrading, hesitation, impatience, hope, fear, loss aversion, and rule drift.

## Livermore Notes

In [How to Trade in Stocks](../source-notes/2026-06-13-how-to-trade-in-stocks-jesse-livermore.md), the recurring psychological errors are:

- Wanting action before the proper entry point appears.
- Hesitating after a danger signal.
- Letting tips or public explanations substitute for price behavior.
- Becoming angry at the market after one's own bad timing.
- Exiting a winner early because the open profit feels psychologically fragile.
- Overtrading after success.

Livermore's antidote is a written process: records, pre-planned points of action, defined risk, and the willingness to stand aside.

## Notes From Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard (Minervini, 2013)

As of publication date 2013. Source: [Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard](../source-notes/2026-06-18-trade-like-a-stock-market-wizard.md).

### Ego vs. Money

The most common reason traders hold losing positions too long is ego — the unwillingness to admit being wrong. Minervini describes a formative experience where fear of ridicule from a broker prevented him from executing sell orders, turning small losses into large ones. The turning point: deciding it was more important to make money than to be right.

**Practical rule:** Separate trading decisions from personal identity. A stopped-out trade is not a personal failure; it is the cost of doing business in a probabilistic environment.

### Do You Want to Be Right or Make Money?

These two goals are often incompatible. Being right requires defending your thesis; making money requires following the market's evidence. When they conflict, money wins. The market is not obligated to confirm your analysis — and the longer you wait for confirmation, the larger the loss.

### Commitment vs. Interest

Minervini distinguishes between *interest* (starting) and *commitment* (persisting through difficulty). Most people get interested in trading; few make an unconditional commitment. The difference shows when the strategy stops working temporarily — committed traders work through it and analyse their failures; interested traders quit or change systems at exactly the wrong moment.

### Paper Trading Limitation

Paper trading (simulating trades without real money) does not develop the emotional circuits needed for real-money trading under financial pressure. Without real consequences, decisions are made without the emotional weight that accompanies actual losses. Recommendation: trade with real money — small but real — as soon as possible. The experience of feeling a real loss is irreplaceable.
