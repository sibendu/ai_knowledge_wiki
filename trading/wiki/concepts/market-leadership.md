# Market Leadership

## Definition

Market leadership is the idea that broad market direction and opportunity often concentrate in a small number of leading stocks, sectors, or groups, and that these leaders rotate over time.

## Why It Matters For Trading

In [How to Trade in Stocks](../source-notes/2026-06-13-how-to-trade-in-stocks-jesse-livermore.md), Livermore argues that it is easier and safer to track a few important groups than to spread attention across the entire market. He also warns that leadership is temporary: the groups leading today may not lead in future cycles.

## Practical Signals

- A group advances while many others are dull or lagging.
- Multiple stocks in the same group confirm the move.
- A prior leader fails to recover while other groups do, suggesting leadership loss.
- Price action changes before the public explanation becomes known.

## Trading Use

- Use leadership as a filter before looking for individual setups.
- Prefer setups in confirmed leaders when trading long momentum or breakouts.
- Watch lagging action in former leaders as a possible warning.
- Avoid turning bullish or bearish on the entire market based on one stock or one group.

## Common Mistakes

- Tracking too many names and losing clarity.
- Assuming a former leader will keep leading.
- Acting on one stock's reversal before group confirmation.
- Waiting for a public explanation instead of respecting price behavior.

## Group Divergence Signal (Reminiscences, 1923)

Source: [Reminiscences of a Stock Operator](../source-notes/2026-06-19-reminiscences-of-a-stock-operator.md). Leadership describes *which* group leads; the related concept of [Stock Group Behavior](stock-group-behavior.md) adds an actionable *divergence* signal: a stock that refuses to follow its group in a bull market is usually being distributed by informed insiders, and the tape warns before the news (Chester Motors, Guiana Gold). The mirror case — a stock supported by inside buying while the tape is weak — flags accumulation. See the dedicated [Stock Group Behavior](stock-group-behavior.md) page for examples and failure modes.

## Notes From Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard (Minervini, 2013)

As of publication date 2013. Source: [Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard](../source-notes/2026-06-18-trade-like-a-stock-market-wizard.md).

### Leaders Emerge First

True market leaders bottom and begin advancing *before* their sector and *before* laggards — often months earlier. Watching for stocks that hold up during market weakness and begin building RS strength while still in a base is the key to identifying leaders before the advance becomes obvious to the crowd.

![Leaders vs. sector vs. laggards timing](../../raw/inbox/trade_like_a_stock_market_wizard/assets/stock-trading/fig_p181_073.png)

*Typical emergence sequence: leaders bottom first and advance earliest, the sector ETF confirms later, and laggards recover last (or not at all).*

### Amazon as a Leadership Example (2001–2003)

Amazon bottomed months before the Nasdaq composite during the 2001–2003 bear market and subsequently advanced 240% from its buy point while most stocks were still declining — a clear illustration of a leader separating from the pack.

![Amazon vs. Nasdaq early bottoming 2001–2003](../../raw/inbox/trade_like_a_stock_market_wizard/assets/stock-trading/fig_p182_074.png)

*Amazon price chart overlaid with the Nasdaq composite: Amazon's early bottoming and advance ahead of the broader index.*

### Leader vs. Laggard Selection

Within a strong sector, buy the leader — the stock with the highest [RS Ranking](../indicators/relative-strength-ranking.md), strongest earnings acceleration, and tightest base structure. Laggards within a strong sector rarely produce superperformance even when the sector is advancing. Buying the "cheap" version of a sector leader is a common value trap.

Example: Whole Foods (the leader at higher P/E) produced vastly superior returns versus Wild Oats (the laggard at lower P/E). The cheaper-multiple stock was a trap, not a bargain.

### Industry Group Leadership

Leading stocks emerge from leading industry groups. New market cycles are characterised by rotation into new leading groups (biotech in 1990, internet in 1999, energy in 2005, growth tech in 2009). Identifying the leading group early, then finding the leading stock within that group, is the framework for maximum impact. Within-sector RS ranking identifies which name is doing the leading.
