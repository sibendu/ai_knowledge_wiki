# Line of Least Resistance

## Definition

The **line of least resistance** is Livermore's term (as narrated in [Reminiscences of a Stock Operator](../source-notes/2026-06-19-reminiscences-of-a-stock-operator.md)) for the direction in which price will move most easily because buying or selling pressure encounters the least opposition. A market that fluctuates within a range has not yet defined its line of least resistance; once price decisively breaks the range, the line is established and the trader should follow it.

> "Prices, like everything else, move along the line of least resistance. They will do whatever comes easiest, therefore they will go up if there is less resistance to an advance than to a decline; and vice versa."

## Why It Matters For Trading

It reframes the entry problem from *predicting* a move to *waiting for the market to reveal* the move. Inside a range, both bulls and bears are trapped near the boundaries; when one boundary breaks, the trapped traders are forced to cover or liquidate, which pushes price further along the same line. The break itself is the signal, not the level.

## How To Identify It

- Watch a stock or commodity that has been oscillating in a defined band (Livermore's wheat example: $1.10–$1.20 for months).
- Do nothing while price is "getting nowhere in particular."
- Treat a decisive close beyond the range boundary as the line defining itself (wheat crossing $1.20; cotton crossing its upper resistance).
- News released after the break is usually *in harmony* with the line already established — "in bull markets bear items are ignored and bull news exaggerated, and vice versa."

## How To Trade It

- **Wait for the break.** Buying at $1.20 after the break is wiser than buying at $1.14 inside the range, because only the break proves direction. "Do you wish to gamble blindly... or speculate intelligently and get a smaller but much more probable profit?"
- **Probe, then add.** Commit a fraction first; the way the market absorbs the order is itself a test of the line (see [Risk Management](risk-management.md) and [Tape Reading](tape-reading.md)).
- **Do not try to start the move yourself.** Livermore's costly cotton lesson: he repeatedly bid cotton up to "help" the break, and each time it sagged back the moment he stopped buying, costing ~$200,000. A real line of least resistance does not need your push.

## Invalidation / Failure Modes

- **Acting before the break:** anticipating the starting signal and entering inside the range (the cotton mistake).
- **Forcing the move:** manufacturing artificial activity to trigger a break that basic conditions do not support.
- **Arguing with the tape after the break:** if price re-enters the range or reverses, the line was not truly established — quit, do not rationalise.
- **Confusing price level with resistance:** "stocks are never too high to buy or too low to sell." The level per se does not define the line; the behaviour of buying vs. selling does.
