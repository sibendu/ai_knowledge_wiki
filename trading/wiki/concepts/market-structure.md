# Market Structure

Market structure describes how trading venues, participants, liquidity, spreads, regulation, and order flow shape price movement and execution.

## Livermore Notes

[How to Trade in Stocks](../source-notes/2026-06-13-how-to-trade-in-stocks-jesse-livermore.md) is not a modern microstructure text, but it contains historically useful market-structure observations:

- Leadership can concentrate in a few groups and rotate over time.
- Large historical commodity and stock operations depended on liquidity and market rules that differ from modern conditions.
- Public explanations often arrive after price action has already changed.
- Short interest can become future buying demand, though modern short-sale rules and position limits change the practical mechanics.

## Historical Structure Notes From Reminiscences (LeFevre, 1923)

Source: [Reminiscences of a Stock Operator](../source-notes/2026-06-19-reminiscences-of-a-stock-operator.md). These describe a **pre-SEC** market and are historical context, not modern microstructure.

- **Bucket shops** let customers bet on tape fluctuations without real exchange orders; the automatic wipe-out at the margin limit functioned as a crude built-in stop-loss, but shops routinely engineered "bucket-shop drives" (brief washed declines) to clear customer margins.
- **Tape lag** was a structural risk: in fast markets the printed ticker ran far behind floor prices (30–40 points during the May 9, 1901 panic), so an "accurate" tape read could still be ruinous.
- **Pools, corners, and matched/wash sales** were common tools of inside cliques and operators; most are now illegal. See [Market Manipulation](market-manipulation.md) for the mechanics and the durable defensive lessons.
- **Own market impact:** a large operator cannot sell at will — "he has to sell when he can, not when he wants to." Liquidity and the character of demand constrain large lines, a point that remains valid today.

## Auction Model and Crowd Dynamics (TA4D, 2020)

Securities markets operate as a continuous auction where supply/demand adjusts in real time — price discovery is dynamic, not static. (source: TA4D 2020)

- **You are a price-taker:** individual traders don't set prices; the crowd does. As Bernard Baruch observed: a cloud of midges moves as one unit with no apparent individual decision-maker — markets work the same way (source: TA4D 2020)
- **Mean reversion tendency:** prices cluster around averages even while trending; straying far from the average is unsustainable and invites correction (source: TA4D 2020)
- **Crowd extremes:** manias and panics push prices far beyond rational valuation; the technical trader accepts this rather than fighting it (source: TA4D 2020)
- **Self-fulfilling mechanics:** widely watched technical levels (200-day MA, round numbers, established S/R) attract buying/selling at those exact levels because many participants act simultaneously — the crowd reinforces the signal (source: TA4D 2020)
- **Efficient markets critique:** if markets were truly efficient, bubbles and crashes would not occur; behavioral economics documents systematic biases (overconfidence, wishful thinking) that create exploitable patterns (source: TA4D 2020)

### Auction vs. Conventional Supply/Demand

In orthodox economics, demand falls as price rises. Securities markets often invert this: visible demand begets more demand, especially in breakout conditions. The eBay auction model (TA4D term) explains breakouts, spikes, and gaps that conventional supply/demand cannot — when prices rise, more buyers pile in, not fewer, generating momentum and emotional escalation (dopamine, adrenaline). (source: TA4D 2020)

### Mean Reversion and Standard Deviation

Prices cluster around a running average; deviations beyond one standard deviation are temporary and tend to revert. Practical implications:

- In a sideways range, buy at the lower band, sell at the upper band
- Mean-reversion trading is most reliable when price has no directional bias; in a trend, the mean itself shifts and the technique requires adjustment
- Tail risk: prices that move multiple standard deviations from the mean signal an abnormal event — either a genuine structural change or an extreme that will snap back (source: TA4D 2020)

### Retracements and Position Squaring

Retracements (also: corrections, pullbacks, throwbacks) occur when the crowd temporarily exhausts its buying or selling power. Key mechanics: (source: TA4D 2020)

- When all willing buyers have bought, the market is overbought and cash-depleted — a retracement follows as traders exit to free capital
- Large institutional players face position limits and must sell even when they expect further gains — "position squaring" creates retracements unrelated to fundamentals
- A cascade of stop-loss orders can amplify a minor retracement into a rout
- No reliable rule exists for predicting retracement depth; common heuristics (prior swing lows, round numbers, 38%/62% ratios) are guidelines only

### Soros Reflexivity

George Soros's concept of reflexivity describes a two-way feedback loop: mania or panic causes abnormal price moves, which are then interpreted by the same crowd as vindication of the original impulse, reinforcing the move further. All market participants have incomplete information, making distorted group narratives self-perpetuating until they collapse. (source: TA4D 2020)
