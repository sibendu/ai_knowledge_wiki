# Earnings Acceleration

**Context:** Growth-stock fundamental analysis
**Used in:** [SEPA Strategy](../strategies/sepa-strategy.md)

## Definition

Earnings acceleration means the **rate of EPS (earnings per share) growth is itself increasing** quarter-over-quarter — not just that earnings are growing, but that they are growing *faster* each successive quarter.

Example of acceleration:
- Q1 YoY: +10%
- Q2 YoY: +28%
- Q3 YoY: +56%

A stock with flat growth rates (+20%, +20%, +20%) is growing but not accelerating.

## Why It Matters for Trading

Minervini's historical study of the biggest stock market winners (drawing on research including Richard Love's *Superperformance Stocks*) found that approximately 90% of the biggest historical winners showed earnings acceleration in the quarters leading up to their largest price advances. Confidence: medium (author's historical analysis; not an independent controlled study).

The rationale: acceleration signals a business inflection point — demand exceeding capacity, operating leverage firing, or a new product expanding faster than expected. Institutional investors model future earnings; unexpected acceleration forces upward estimate revisions, drawing in more buyers. This is self-reinforcing until the acceleration peaks.

## Minimum Thresholds (Minervini's Guidelines)

| Metric | Minimum threshold | Ideal for superperformers |
|--------|------------------|--------------------------|
| Quarterly EPS growth (YoY) | ≥20–25% | 40–100%+ in ≥2 recent quarters |
| Quarterly sales growth (YoY) | Positive acceleration | Triple-digit in strongest cases |
| Sequence length | ≥2 consecutive quarters of acceleration | 3–4+ quarters preferred |

## Sales Acceleration

Earnings acceleration should be accompanied by **sales (revenue) acceleration** — the growth rate in sales also increasing quarter-to-quarter. Earnings growth driven purely by cost cuts without corresponding sales growth is lower quality and less sustainable.

Monster Beverage (MNST) 2003–2006 is the book's primary example of sustained acceleration across all three metrics (EPS, sales, and margin) simultaneously — see Code 33 in [Earnings Quality](../concepts/earnings-quality.md).

## Earnings Maturation Cycle

Earnings acceleration does not last forever. The typical arc for a growth stock:

1. **Early acceleration:** EPS growth rate increasing quarter after quarter — stock often undiscovered
2. **Peak growth:** Very high absolute EPS growth; stock widely followed by institutions
3. **Deceleration begins:** Growth rate still positive but slowing — the market begins pricing in the peak ahead of time
4. **Estimate disappointments:** Guidance cuts, earnings misses; price often tops before EPS turns negative
5. **Earnings decline:** Stage 3–4 price behaviour confirmed

Minervini's observation: stocks consistently top *before* earnings turn negative — the market discounts deceleration, not just contraction. Watching for the first sign of a slowing growth rate (not just a miss, but a lower growth rate than prior quarters) is the key fundamental sell signal.

**Dell Computer example:** EPS grew at 80%+ in the mid-1990s, then decelerated to 65%, then 28%, then single digits. The stock declined 80% from its high by 2010 — long before EPS went negative.

## Earnings Estimate Revisions

Analyst estimate revisions amplify price moves:
- **Upward revisions** (price target increases) attract institutional buying — the most powerful tailwind for price
- **Downward revisions** trigger institutional selling
- The fastest-moving stocks are often those where estimates are being revised upward consistently

## Practical Signals

| Signal | Interpretation |
|--------|---------------|
| 2+ consecutive quarters of accelerating EPS and sales growth | Potential Stage 2 entry candidate |
| First quarter where growth rate slows (even if still high) | Begin watching for Stage 3 signs |
| Earnings miss + guidance cut | High-probability Stage 3 signal; tighten stops |
| Two consecutive quarters of decelerating growth | Strong sell signal regardless of absolute EPS level |

## Common Mistakes

- Buying a stock with high absolute EPS growth (e.g., +35%) without checking whether the growth rate is accelerating or decelerating
- Ignoring sales growth quality (cost-cutting vs. genuine demand)
- Holding through EPS deceleration because "the stock still has great earnings" — the market has already begun discounting the slowing
