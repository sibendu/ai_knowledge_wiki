# Livermore Trading Principles

## Short Answer

Livermore's books reduce to a disciplined trend-following philosophy: wait for a major price-and-time signal, act promptly when the market confirms, add only to winners, protect capital, and stay mentally detached enough to obey danger signals. The 1940 *How to Trade in Stocks* states the rules; the 1923 *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator* supplies the stories and reasoning behind them.

## Core Principles

1. Speculation is a business. It requires study, records, rules, and emotional balance.
2. Do not trade constantly. Wait for periods when a meaningful movement is likely.
3. Price action is the judge. A thesis matters only if the market confirms it.
4. Focus on leaders. Track a limited number of important groups and accept that leaders rotate.
5. Enter near pivotal points. A well-timed entry creates the profit cushion needed to sit through normal reactions.
6. Add only to winners. Long additions should occur at higher prices; short additions should occur at lower prices.
7. Never average down. A losing first trade is information, not an invitation to increase risk.
8. Keep profits real. Take some money out after successful campaigns so gains are not just account marks.
9. Beware tips. Inside information can be incomplete, stale, biased, or useless for exits.
10. Keep records. Records help filter noise, identify pivotal points, and reduce dependence on opinion.

## Principles Emphasised In Reminiscences (1923)

Source: [Reminiscences of a Stock Operator](../source-notes/2026-06-19-reminiscences-of-a-stock-operator.md).

1. **Sitting tight is the hard part.** "It never was my thinking that made the big money for me. It always was my sitting." Being right is common; *holding* a correct position through normal reactions is rare and is where the big money is made. This is the lesson of **Old Turkey (Mr. Partridge)**, who answered every tip with "It's a bull market, you know!" — meaning the money is in the main movement, and selling a winning position in a bull market means risking the loss of your *position*.
2. **The big money is in the big swing, not the fluctuations.** Jumping in and out to catch every wiggle is fatal; study general conditions and ride the main current.
3. **Follow the [line of least resistance](../concepts/line-of-least-resistance.md).** Wait for price to break its range before committing to a direction; do not anticipate or force the break.
4. **Probe, then pyramid.** Risk a small exploratory bet first; add size only when the position proves you right. "Have the big bet down only when you win."
5. **Always sell the loser, keep the winner.** Averaging a losing trade is "among the greatest of all speculative blunders." Livermore's costliest violation: selling profitable wheat while holding losing cotton (the Percy Thomas episode).
6. **Reverse hope and fear.** Fear that a small loss will become a big loss; hope that a profit will become a bigger one — the opposite of the natural human impulse.
7. **Read behaviour, not tips.** The tape (and group behaviour) warns before the news; tips are useless because the giver controls neither your entry nor your exit.
8. **There is a time to do nothing.** "The Wall Street fool thinks he must trade all the time." Standing aside is a position.

## Modern Translation

- Replace fixed point thresholds with volatility-adjusted or percentage thresholds.
- Test any rule against liquid, split-adjusted, survivorship-bias-aware data.
- Add explicit stop, sizing, and portfolio exposure rules.
- Treat the book as a source of hypotheses, not a validated trading system.
