# Livermore Market Key

## Definition

The Livermore Market Key is a manual price-recording method intended to filter minor fluctuations and highlight trend changes, natural rallies, natural reactions, and pivotal points.

## Construction

From [How to Trade in Stocks](../source-notes/2026-06-13-how-to-trade-in-stocks-jesse-livermore.md), the method uses columns for:

- Upward Trend.
- Downward Trend.
- Natural Rally.
- Natural Reaction.
- Secondary Rally.
- Secondary Reaction.

Livermore used different ink/pencil markings to distinguish confirmed trend entries from natural oscillations. For individual stocks around $30 or higher, he used approximately six points to define a natural rally or reaction, and three points of follow-through beyond a prior pivotal point as confirmation. For a combined group "Key Price," he used larger thresholds.

## Interpretation

- The last price in an Upward Trend or Downward Trend column becomes a pivotal point once a natural reaction or rally starts.
- After two pivotal points exist, the next approach to those levels becomes important.
- A resumed move should carry through the prior pivotal point by the required threshold.
- Failure to carry through becomes a danger signal.
- Confirmation should come from more than one stock in a group when possible.

## Trading Use Cases

- Identify when a pullback is normal versus potentially trend-ending.
- Decide whether a breakout has enough follow-through.
- Compare two leading stocks in the same group to reduce false signals.
- Keep the trader focused on major movements instead of day-to-day noise.

## Failure Modes

- The point thresholds are historically specific and should be normalized for modern prices and volatility.
- The method uses end-of-day style record keeping and may not map cleanly to intraday trading.
- One-stock signals can be false; Livermore preferred group confirmation.
- The PDF's example tables did not fully extract as text, so implementation details should be checked visually from the original pages before coding.
