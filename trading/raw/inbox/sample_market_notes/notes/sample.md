## Technical Analysis Masterclass

## Chapter 1: Breakout Patterns

In technical analysis, a breakout occurs when price moves above a resistance level or below a support level. Breakouts are often accompanied by increased volume, which confirms the validity of the move. The chart below demonstrates a classic ascending triangle breakout with volume confirmation.

BTC/USD 4H Chart · Ascending Triangle Breakout

Resistance

Volume

Notice how the RSI indicator (bottom panel) confirms the breakout by crossing above 60 simultaneously with the price breakout. This dual confirmation increases the probability of a successful trade.

![[sample/fig_p1_001_vec.png]]

> [!figure]
> **BTC/USD 4H Ascending Triangle Breakout with Volume Confirmation**
>
> A 4-hour candlestick chart of BTC/USD showing an ascending triangle pattern culminating in a breakout above a horizontal resistance level marked by an orange dashed line. The price candles show progressively higher lows while repeatedly testing the same resistance ceiling. The final two candles show a large green candle breaking above resistance and a subsequent red candle still above the resistance line. A volume panel below the price chart shows increasing volume bars over the sequence, with the breakout candle (large green bar) exhibiting the highest volume spike, confirming the breakout.
>
> **Key insights:**
> - An ascending triangle is characterized by a flat horizontal resistance line and a rising trendline of higher lows, indicating accumulating bullish pressure.
> - A valid breakout occurs when price closes above the resistance level, as shown by the large green candle piercing the orange dashed resistance line.
> - Breakout validity is confirmed by significantly increased volume at the moment of the breakout, visible as the tallest volume bar in the lower panel.
> - After a breakout candle, a red (bearish) candle that stays above the former resistance level suggests the resistance has been successfully converted to support.
> - The BTC/USD 4H timeframe demonstrates that ascending triangle breakouts can be identified across multiple candles of coiling price action before resolution.

Support and Resistance Levels

Support and resistance are key price levels where buying or selling pressure historically has been significant. The diagram below shows how these levels act as floors and ceilings for price action.

### Support & Resistance · Role Reversal Example

## Resistance Level

· Former resistance    becomes support

## Support Level

Key insight: Once a resistance level is broken, it often becomes the new support (role reversal). This is a fundamental concept that traders use to identify re-entry points after a breakout.

![[sample/fig_p2_002_vec.png]]

> [!figure]
> **Support & Resistance Role Reversal Example**
>
> The chart illustrates the concept of role reversal in technical analysis using two horizontal lines and a price line. A blue horizontal line at the bottom represents the Support Level, and a red horizontal line in the middle represents the Resistance Level. A blue price line oscillates near the support level, bounces twice, then rallies upward to break through the resistance level. After breaking through, the price (now shown in green) pulls back slightly to the former resistance level, then continues rising sharply, demonstrating that the former resistance has become new support. The annotation 'Former resistance becomes support' marks this transition point.
>
> **Key insights:**
> - A resistance level acts as a ceiling that price struggles to break above.
> - A support level acts as a floor that prevents price from falling further.
> - When price breaks above a resistance level, that former resistance level often becomes a new support level — a phenomenon known as role reversal.
> - After a breakout above resistance, a pullback to the former resistance level that holds as support confirms the role reversal.
> - Role reversal is a key principle in technical analysis used to identify new support and resistance zones after significant price moves.

The Relative Strength Index (RSI)

RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of price changes. It oscillates between 0 and 100. Readings above 70 indicate overbought conditions, while readings below 30 indicate oversold conditions.

RSI(14) · Divergence Signal

## 50 Bearish Divergence ·

RSI divergence occurs when price makes a new high but RSI makes a lower high. This bearish divergence signals potential trend reversal and is a powerful warning signal for traders holding long positions.

![[sample/fig_p3_003_vec.png]]

> [!figure]
> **RSI(14) Bearish Divergence Signal Chart**
>
> A line chart displaying the RSI(14) indicator with three key horizontal reference levels: overbought at 70 (red dashed line), midpoint at 50 (gray dashed line), and oversold at 30 (blue dashed line). The purple RSI line shows two peaks — a higher first peak near 70 and a lower second peak around 55 — connected by an orange dashed trendline labeled 'Bearish Divergence.' The RSI line trends downward after the second peak, approaching the oversold level of 30 by the right edge of the chart. The pattern illustrates how RSI can make lower highs even as price may be making higher highs.
>
> **Key insights:**
> - Bearish divergence on RSI occurs when the RSI makes a lower high on its second peak compared to its first peak, signaling weakening upward momentum.
> - An RSI reading above 70 indicates overbought conditions, while below 30 indicates oversold conditions.
> - In this example, the RSI first peak approaches 70 (overbought), but the second peak only reaches approximately 55, forming a bearish divergence pattern.
> - After a bearish divergence signal, the RSI in this chart trends sharply downward toward the oversold zone (30), suggesting a potential price decline.
> - Bearish divergence is identified by drawing a trendline connecting the two RSI highs — a downward-sloping line despite price potentially still rising.
